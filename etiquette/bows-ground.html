Bows to the Ground and from the Waist

Bows during Divine services express worship to the Lord, reverence toward the Theotokos, and veneration of the saints. Orthodox practice uses both full prostrations to the ground and smaller bows from the waist.

At a glance

  • Great bows are more common in fasting seasons, especially Great Lent, and at certain prayers.
  • Waist bows are typical for most of the year.
  • If health or space is limited, make a reverent waist bow. God sees the intention.
  • Children and the elderly may always use the waist bow.

The three types of bows

  • Ordinary bow to the chest or navel
  • Middle bow from the waist
  • Great bow to the ground (full prostration)

Technique for a great bow

Do not strike or press your head to the floor. Bend the knees, lower the head gently, and if customary, touch the prayer rug lightly. Bring the hands from the heart together, place them on the rug, and keep elbows close. In services, bow and rise together with the people at the rector’s direction.

About the quick “throwing” bow

A rapid form sometimes called a throwing bow is done in quick succession without lowering the head fully to the rug.

Practical notes

  • Follow the local typikon and the priest’s cues. Bow with the congregation.
  • Keep movements calm and modest. Use a prayer rug where customary.
  • If movement is limited, a simple head inclination is sufficient.
